The Enigma Machine
Arthur Scherbius, a German, originally developed the Enigma Machine so he could keep his business information private. It was also built to attract companies interested in secure communications. He set up the Cipher Machines Corporation in 1923 so his Enigma Machines could be produced commercially. The German army, navy, and air force later began manufacturing their own Enigma Machines.
(Ashish, The Imitation Game: How Did The Enigma Machine Work?, 2019)
(Andrew Lycett, Breaking Germany’s Enigma Code, 2011)


Enigma Machine, How Alan Turing Cracked the Enigma Code.



“The machine consists of box 26 keys labeled [sic] with the letters of the alphabet and 26 bulbs which shine through stencils on which letters are marked. It also contains wheels… When a key is depressed the keys are made to move in a certain way and the current flows to the bulbs. The letter which appears over the bulb is the result of enciphering the letter depressed key with the wheels in the position they have when the bulb lights.”
(Alan Turing, Mathematical Theory of ENIGMA Machine, 1942)


“The wheels include one which is seldom removed from the machine, and which may or may not be rotatable. It is called the Umkeh rwalz [sic](U.K.W.)... There are three or more other wheels which are removable and rotatable…” 1 The Enigma Machine had three (later five) wheels as well as a ten plugs to scramble the path of the current further. 2
1(Alan Turing, Mathematical Theory of ENIGMA Machine, 1942)
2(Harold C. Deutsch, The Historical Impact of Revealing The Ultra Secret, 2006)

By creating a copy of the basic German enciphering machine, the Poles managed to read German signal traffic throughout the 1930s with varying degrees of success. However, shortly before the Munich conference in September 1938, the Germans made alterations to their enciphering machine–the so-called Enigma machine–and in mid-September, darkness closed over German message traffic.”
(Williamson Murray Ultra–The Misunderstood Allied Secret Weapon, 2002)

Polish Developments
“After the First World War, Poland reestablished itself as an independent state, but it was concerned about the threats to its newfound sovereignty. To the east lay Russia, a nation ambitions to spread its communism, and to the west lay Germany, desperate to regain territory ceded to Poland after the war. Sandwiched between these two enemies, the Poles were desperate for intelligence information, and they formed a new cipher bureau, the Biuro Szyfrów. If necessity is the mother of invention, then perhaps adversity is the mother of cryptanalysis... Their monitoring of German communications had been… effective, until 1926, when they too encountered the Enigma messages.”
(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


“In charge of deciphering the German messages [for Poland] was Captain Maksymilian Ciezki… Ciezki had access to a commercial version of the Enigma machine, which revealed all the principles of Scherbius’s invention. Unfortunately, the commercial version was distinctly different from the military one in terms or the wirings inside each scrambler.” Later, intelligence from a spy, Hans-Thilo Schmidt, gave Poland the wirings of the military machine.
(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


“For centuries, it had been assumed that the best cryptanalysts were experts in the structure of language, but the arrival of Enigma prompted the Poles to alter their recruiting policy. Enigma was a mechanical cipher, and the Biuro Szyfrów reasoned that a scientific mind might stand a better chance of breaking it.” Twenty mathematicians from a once German university, therefore fluent in German, were selected and sworn to secrecy. Three that showed skill in cryptanalysis, including Marian Rejewski, were invited into the Biuro Szyfrów, the Polish code-breaking center.
(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


In 1932 and 1933, Marian Rejewski used photographs and an operating manual from French Intelligence to deduce the wiring of the Enigma Machine. He learned Enigma operators set the three wheels inside to specific starting positions that other operators were informed of. Positions were set in place before the message was enciphered and he produced a method to find those starting positions, the Bomba. 1 The Bomba “was at first an electro-mechanical and later an electronic computer that was aimed to adjust itself to whatever alterations the Germans might make in the… Enigma mechanism… By 1937 its Polish inventors had improved it to a point where, for a time, they read three-fourths of all Enigma messages.” 2 However, in May 1940, the Enigma Machine underwent a major change so Rejewski’s method no longer worked. 1
1(B.J. Copeland, Ultra, 2019)
2(Harold C. Deutsch, The Historical Impact of Revealing The Ultra Secret, 2006)


“[The Poles] set up house with the French and some Spanish exiles at Vignolles, near Paris. The Poles, in fact, favored a single facility to include the British, but on this the French were adamantly negative. There was, however, an exchange of technical data and deciphered messages with the central British decrypting establishment at Bletchley Park, where the British worked by themselves until the Americans joined them after entering the war late in 1941.”
(Harold C. Deutsch, The Historical Impact of Revealing The Ultra Secret, 2006)


Hans-Thilo Schmidt
In 1888, Hans-Thilo Schmidt was born in Berlin. “Schmidt embarked on a career in the German Army and fought in the First World War, but he was not considered worthy enough to remain in the army... He attempted to become a businessman, but his soap factory was forced to close because of the postwar depression and hyperinflation... The humiliation of Schmidt’s failures was compounded by the success of his elder brother, Rudolph, who had also fought in the war, and retained in the army afterward... He was [eventually] responsible for ensuring secure communications and in fact it was Rudolph who officially sanctioned the army’s use of the Enigma cipher. After [Schmidt’s] business collapsed… Rudolph arranged a job for him in Berlin at the Chiffrierstelle", the department dealing with Enigma. Starting his new job, Schmidt moved to “expensive Berlin, impoverished and isolated, envious of his perfect brother and resentful toward a nation which had rejected him. The result was inevitable. By selling secret Enigma information to foreign powers. Hans-Thilo Schmidt could earn money and gain revente, damagins his country’s security and undermining his brother’s organization.”
(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


“The case officer assigned to Schmidt was another German citizen working for the French and using the name Lemoine, code named 'Rex', who would meet with Schmidt to obtain the information he provided, including daily Enigma ciphers for certain months.” 1 On November 8, 1931, “in exchange for” $30,000 as of today, Schmidt allowed Rex to photograph two documents [that] were essentially instructions for using the Enigma machine, and although there was no explicit description of the wirings inside each scrambler, they contained the information needed to deduce those wirings.” 2 Unable to shake the French Cipher Section of the Army General Staff from its lethargy and ignorance of the threat presented by the complex German encryption capability, French Intelligence officers passed the information on to the Polish Biuro Szyfrów [sic]” 1 to support an agreement of military cooperation the French and Polish signed earlier. 2 “With the help of Schmidt’s pilfered ciphers, the brilliant Polish mathematician and cryptologist Marian Rejewski was able to provide the calculations needed to successfully replicate an Enigma machine in early 1933. Thereafter, work began in earnest to develop further calculations that would enable the machine to actually decipher intercepted German messages, but it was an excruciatingly slow process that would only begin to come close to its goal in 1937 with the refining of an electromechanical process known as the ‘Bomba’” 1
1(Edward J. Hagerty and Paul Paillole, Journal of Strategic Security, 2017)
2(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


Hans-Thilo Schmdit’s espionage was crucial to the Allies’ success because he provided Poland with information about the Enigma Machine. Without it, the Allies wouldn’t know how the Enigma Machine worked.

Encryption Flaws
“A basic flaw in the design of Enigma was that no letter of the alphabet could ever be represented by itself once enciphered - a could never be a, z could never be z. Together with errors in sent messages and the German habit of using standard phrases at the start of each communication, this allowed cryptanalysts to establish informed ‘guesses’. Nevertheless, without a swift mechanical deciphering system, Enigma’s 150,000,000,000,000,000,000 combinations were practically unbreakable.”
(National Archives, Secrets & spies)


Besides the flaws of the machine, human error also contributed to the Allies’ success. “For each message, the operator was supposed to select a different message key, three letters chosen at random. However, in the heat of battle, rather than straining their imaginations to pick a random key, the overworked operators would sometimes pick three consecutive letters from the Enigma keyboard… These predictable message keys became known as cillies.” Operators occasionally used the same key multiple times. That’s why codebreakers would check the cillies every day.
(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


Enigma Machine Wiring, 2006, The Imitation Game: How Did The Enigma Machine Work?



The Nazis also implemented rules that supposedly would make the Enigma harder to crack, but actually reduced the permutations. “Excluding certain arrangements to avoid a scrambler remaining in the same position meant that the codebook compilers reduced by half the number of possible scrambler arrangements.” When arrangements were deducted for one day, they could eliminate half the arrangements during the next day. Another rule was “that the plugboard settings could not include a swap between any letter and its neighbor, which meant that S could be swapped with any letter except R and T.” Although this avoided obvious settings, it eliminated many possibilities.
(Simon Singh, The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography, 1999)


These loopholes gave Bletchley Park a starting point, and eliminated possibilities that saved them time, also helping improve code-breaking technology like the Bombe.


The Bombe
“Some keys would be broken within 2-4 hours, some would never be broken – speed was always of the essence… At [Bletchley] Park, Alan Turing was asked to find a way to break Enigma messages. Because of changes to the German operating procedures and the introduction of extra wheels, the Polish Bomba was now obsolete… Throughout the war the operation built around 211 Bombe machines and broke many keys on a daily basis. Huge amounts of intercepted traffic were deciphered, supplying invaluable information about enemy operations… The first Turing/Welchman Bombe based machine, known as Agnus Dei or simply Agnes, became operational in August 1940.”
(The National Museum of Computing, The Turing-Welchman Bombe)


The Enigma Machine was built “with nearly 12 miles of wiring and 97,000 carefully machined parts.”
(Ashish, The Imitation Game: How Did The Enigma Machine Work?, 2019)


The Turing-Welsh Bombe Machine, The Turing-Welchman Bombe.



“It was the task of the Bombe to discover the daily key - wheel order, wheel settings and plugboard configuration - to enable the 3-5,000 Enigma messages intercepted each day to be deciphered" 1 by searching “through different possible positions of Enigma’s internal wheels, looking for a pattern of keyboard-to-lamp board connections that would turn coded letters into plain German. The method depended on human instinct, though; to initiate the process, a code breaker had to guess a few words in the message (these guessed words were called a crib). The Polish Bomba, a simpler 18-drum machine, was a forerunner of the Bombe, but it was based on Rejewski’s method for finding the wheel positions at the start of the message. Unlike Rejewski’s method, the more powerful crib-based method invented by Turing survived the May 1940 change. 2”
1(The National Museum of Computing, The Turing-Welchman Bombe)
2(B.J. Copeland, Ultra, 2019)
